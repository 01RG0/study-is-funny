## PART 1: INTRODUCTION TO ENGINEERING SCIENCES

### 1.1 What is Engineering?
- **Definition**: The application of mathematics, empirical evidence, and scientific knowledge to invent, innovate, design, build, maintain, and improve structures, machines, tools, systems, and materials
- **Etymology**: Derived from Latin "ingenium" (cleverness) and "ingeniare" (to contrive)
- **Core Principle**: Engineers are innovators, designers, decision-makers, and leaders who solve practical problems

### 1.2 Engineering Fields
**Traditional Fields:**
- Civil Engineering (structural, water resources, environmental)
- Mechanical Engineering (thermal, vehicle, manufacturing)
- Electrical Engineering (power, telecommunications, electronics)
- Chemical Engineering (chemical processes, materials)
- Petroleum Engineering (drilling, reservoir development)

**Non-Traditional Fields:**
- Bioengineering
- Environmental Engineering
- Energy & Renewable Energy Engineering
- Materials Engineering
- Construction Engineering
- Genetic Engineering

### 1.3 Responsibilities of Engineers
1. Safety of the public
2. Welfare of society
3. Environmental concerns
4. Compliance with laws, regulations, standards, and ethical codes
5. Produce products/devices with:
   - Adequate performance
   - Cost-efficiency
   - Low operating and maintenance costs
   - Durability/longevity

### 1.4 Skills Required for Engineering Students
- Strong interest in mathematics and science
- Social awareness and concern for quality of life
- Ability to put ideas into action
- Strong academic background
- Determination and commitment to continuing education
- Excellent communication skills
- Teamwork abilities

### 1.5 STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics)
**Definition**: Integrated approach combining these four disciplines

**Variations:**
- eSTEM: Environmental STEM
- STEAM: Adding Art to STEM
- STREM: Adding Robotics to STEM

| Electric Current | Ampere | A |
| Thermodynamic Temperature | Kelvin | K |
| Amount of Substance | Mole | mol |
| Luminous Intensity | Candela | cd |

### 2.2 Historical Development of SI

**Ancient Period (2750 B.C.)**
- Egyptians used "Cubit" as first recorded standard measurement
- Great Pyramid of Giza built with 440 cubits (21 inches) on each side
- Mean error: only 1.5mm in length!
- Cubits subdivided into: hands, feet, paces

**Modern Period**
- **1799**: French Revolution - platinum standards deposited in Paris Archives
- **1832**: Gauss promoted Metric System using decimal system
- **1860**: Maxwell and Thomson developed coherent system concept
- **1874**: CGS (Centimetre-Gram-Second) system introduced by BAAS
- **1880s**: Electric units added (ohm, volt, ampere)
- **1889**: First CGPM sanctioned international prototypes (meter & kilogram)
- **1901**: Giovanni Giorgi proposed combining mechanical and electrical units
- **1939-1946**: Adoption of four-dimensional system (meter, kilogram, second, ampere)
- **1954**: Addition of ampere, kelvin, and candela as base units
- **1960**: Name "International System of Units (SI)" officially adopted
- **1971**: Mole added as seventh base unit

## PART 3: COMMUNICATION ENGINEERING

### 3.1 Definition of Communication
**Communication** is the transfer of information (data) from a source to a destination over a transmission channel, focusing on the ability to convey information quickly and efficiently in forms of:
- Sound (audio signals)
- Images (video signals)
- Data (digital signals)

### 3.2 General Communication System Components

**Block Diagram Elements:**
1. **Source**: Generates information/message
2. **Transmitter**: Converts information to signal suitable for transmission
3. **Transmission Channel**: Physical medium for signal transmission
4. **Receiver**: Converts transmitted signal back to required information
5. **Destination**: End user

### 3.3 Communication Channels Classification

**A. WIRELINE CHANNELS**

1. **Copper Wire (Twisted Pair)**
   - History: Telephone lines installed 1870s
   - Use: Telephone networks, data cables
   - Bandwidth: Limited (several hundred kHz)
   - Distance: Short
   - Cost: Low
   - Installation: Easy

2. **Coaxial Cable**
   - Invented: 1929 at AT&T Bell Labs
   - First use: 1940s for telephone exchanges
   - 1950s: Television signal transmission
   - Bandwidth: Several MHz
   - Distance: Medium
   - Cost: Medium
   - Installation: Medium difficulty

3. **Waveguide**
   - Type: Hollow metal tube (rectangular or circular)
   - Bandwidth: Very High (GHz range - microwave)
   - History: Proposed late 1800s by Lord Rayleigh
   - Use: Radar, satellite communications
   - Distance: Short
   - Cost: High

4. **Optical Fiber Cable**
   - Concept: Uses light to transmit data
   - History: 1970s - Corning Glass Works development
   - Bandwidth: Extremely High
   - Distance: Long (preferred for intercontinental connections)
   - Cost: Highest
   - Installation: Most difficult

**B. WIRELESS CHANNELS**

**Frequency Bands:**
- Radio Frequency (RF): Traditional wireless (radio, TV, cellular, Wi-Fi)
- Microwave: Satellite, point-to-point links, radar
- Optical Frequency: Short-range (remote controls, LiFi)

**Propagation Environments:**
- Indoor: Influenced by walls, furniture, people
- Outdoor: Affected by terrain and atmospheric conditions

### 3.4 Message Signals

**Analog Messages**
- Continuous signals (audio, video)
- Values vary over continuous range
- Used in early communication systems

**Digital Messages**
- Discrete signals (0s and 1s)
- Represent information as binary digits
- Modern communication standard

### 3.5 Carrier Modulation

**Definition**: Process where a message signal modulates a carrier signal

**Carrier Signal Formula**: f(t) = A·sin(ωt + φ)
- A = amplitude
- ω = frequency
- φ = phase

**Modulation Techniques:**

| Analog Modulation | Digital Modulation |
|---|---|
| Amplitude Modulation (AM) | Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK) |
| Frequency Modulation (FM) | Frequency Shift Keying (FSK) |
| Phase Modulation (PM) | Phase Shift Keying (PSK) |

**Why Modulation?**
1. **Ease of Transmission**: Shifts frequency to match channel bandwidth
2. **Ease of Radiation**: Reduces required antenna size
   - Speech signals: 100-3000 Hz (wavelength: 100-3000 km)
   - Modulated carrier: Much smaller wavelength
3. **Transmission of Multiple Signals**: Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM)

### 3.6 Analog vs Digital Communication Systems

**Analog Systems:**
- Transfer continuous information
- Uses analog modulation techniques
- Used in terrestrial radio and TV broadcasting
- Components: Modulator (transmitter), Demodulator (receiver)

**Digital Systems:**
- Information in digital format (binary digits/bits)
- Message encoded as sequence of 0s and 1s
- More noise-immune than analog
- Uses regenerative repeaters
- Components: A/D converter, encoder, modulator (transmitter side)
              Demodulator, decoder, D/A converter (receiver side)

### 3.7 Advantages of Digital Over Analog

**Noise Immunity:**
- Digital decisions belong to finite-sized alphabet
- Fine details of pulse shape not critical
- Data recovery possible with modest distortion

**Regenerative Repeaters:**
- Incoming pulses detected and retransmitted
- Prevents accumulation of noise and distortion
- Maintains signal integrity over long distances

## PART 4: EVOLUTION OF COMMUNICATION SYSTEMS

### 4.1 Ancient Communication Systems

**Cave Paintings (30,000 B.C.)**
- Oldest communication method
- Signs and symbols recorded events and stories
- Example: Chauvet Cave in France

**Smoke and Drum Signals**
- Simple meanings: "safe" or "danger"
- Form of alarm system
- Limited geographic area

**Postal Systems (6th Century BCE)**
- Persian Emperor Cyrus the Great established first system
- Later: Egypt, Rome, China developed their own
- Enabled empire-wide communication

**Pigeon Post (5th Century BCE)**
- Messages attached to pigeons
- Pigeons returned to home nests
- First system established in Persia and Syria

**Flag Semaphore (15th Century)**
- Hand-held flag positions represent letters/numbers
- Used for ship-to-ship communication

### 4.2 Telegraph Systems

**Optical Telegraph (1790)**
- Created by French brothers (Chappe brothers)
- First European telecommunications system
- Operation: Mechanical rotating arms encoded messages into symbols
- Stations: 10-15 km apart
- Relay: Symbol transmission between towers
- Duration: ~60 years until electrical telegraph

**Electrical Telegraph (1838)**
- Samuel Morse + Alfred Vail + Leonard Gale
- Discovery: Messages sent by holding/releasing buttons in intervals
- **Morse Code**: Combinations of long/short pulses represent letters
- Key milestone: First political support for implementation
- Timeline:
  - 1858: First transatlantic cable (England-US)
  - Late 1800s: Radio telegraphy (wireless Morse code)
  - Turn of 1900s: All long-distance communications depended on telegraph

### 4.3 Telephone Systems

**History of Telephone Development**
- **1672**: Robert Hooke created acoustic telephone
- **1849**: Antonio Meucci (Italy) began telephone development
- **1861**: Johann Philipp Reis (Germany) designed sound transmission instruments
- **1870s**: Elisha Gray and Alexander Graham Bell independently designed electric transmission devices
- **1876**: Bell patented first electric telephone device

**Bell Telephone Company**
- **1882**: Established by Bell and others
- **Later**: AT&T
- Introduced hierarchical network structure for intercity calls

**Network Milestones:**
- **1915**: First coast-to-coast call (land-line)
- **1927**: First radio-telephone service (UK-US across Atlantic)
- **1934**: Service established (US-Japan across Pacific)
- **1956**: First transatlantic telephone cable (Newfoundland-Scotland)
- **Early 1960s**: First digital voice transmission link

**Special Innovation:**
- **Photophone (1880)**: Bell's light-based communication device
  - Transmitted sound in light beam
  - Preceded radio by ~20 years
  - Too advanced for its time; recognized value after fiber optics (1970s)

### 4.4 Television Broadcasting

**Principles:**
- System for broadcasting moving images and sound
- Powerful tool for information, entertainment, education
- Audio/video via microphone and camera → electronic signals
- Delivery: Radio waves (terrestrial), satellite, cable TV, or IPTV
- Reception: Antennas (terrestrial), dishes (satellite), cable boxes

**Television Evolution:**
- **1920s**: Early developments by Phillip T. Farnsworth and John Logie Baird
  - Baird: First public TV broadcast (mechanical scanning disc)
  - Farnsworth: First electronic TV prototype

- **1920s-1950s**: Color TV development (standardized by 1950s)

- **1970s**: Video Cassette Recorders (VCRs) - allowed time-shift viewing

- **1980s**: 
  - Cable and satellite TV (wider channel selection)
  - Stereo sound introduction
  - Traditional network dominance challenged

- **1990s-2000s**: 
  - Digital Television (DTV) - analog to digital transition
  - High-Definition Television (HDTV)
  - New TV standard established (2000s)

- **2010s-Present**: 
  - Smart TVs with internet connectivity
  - IPTV (Internet Protocol Television)
  - Interactive features

### 5.2 The Nile River System

**Key Facts & Figures:**

**Geographic:**
- World's longest river: 6,650 km
- Shared by 11 countries
- Drainage basin: 10% of African continent
- Population: 250 million (2015), 300 million projected (2025)

**Water Resources - Egypt:**
- Total: 81 km³
  - Nile: 55.5 km³
  - Reuse: 22.0 km³
  - Groundwater: 2.0 km³
  - Treated wastewater reuse: 1.0 km³
  - Other: 0.5 km³

**Water Usage - Egypt:**
- Agriculture: 71.5 km³ (87%)
- Domestic: 5.5 km³
- Industry: 3.0 km³
- Navigation: 1.0 km³

**Flow Distribution:**
- Ethiopia (tributaries): 86-95% of flow at Aswan
  - Blue Nile: 56%
  - Atbara: 14%
  - Sobat: 16%
  - Other sources: 14%

**Egypt Water Characteristics:**
- Minimal rainfall
- No flow additions
- 90% population on 5% of Nile land
- Uses ~86% of Nile waters

**Nile Basin Challenges:**
- Limited flow system-wide (~84 bcm/yr)
- Very limited infrastructure
  - 10% hydropower potential developed
  - 15% population with electricity
  - <10% irrigable land irrigated (excluding Egypt & Sudan)
- Region of extremes (poverty, climate variability, conflict)

### 5.6 Hydropower Calculations

**Power Formula:**
P = ρ × g × Q × h

Where:
- ρ = water density (kg/m³)
- g = gravity (m/s²)
- Q = discharge (m³/s)
- h = head/pressure (m)

**For Egyptian system:**
- Unit: Kilowatts (divide by 1.36)
- Unit: Horsepower (divide by 75)

**Energy:**
E = P × t

Where:
- E = Energy (kWh)
- P = Power (kW)
- t = Time (hours)

## PART 6: TEXTILE ENGINEERING

### 6.1 History of Textile Craft

**Ancient Egyptian Period:**
- Textile craft: oldest craft for humans
- Spinner and weaving loom: oldest techniques
- **Process**: 
  1. Collect fibers in ball form
  2. Draw fibers in groups (control thickness)
  3. Twist fibers together while drawing
  4. Wind yarn on bone or wood pieces
  5. Rotate spindle for yarn cohesion
- **Methods of rotation**: Friction with thigh or hanging on tree
- **Spindle weights**: Gypsum, bone, or clay (increase inertia)

**Timeline:**
- 4400 B.C.: Egyptians made woven fabrics
- 2000+ B.C.: Chinese made fine silk fabrics

### 6.2 Classification of Fibers

**Natural Fibers:**
- **Plant (Cellulosic)**:
  - Cotton (from cotton plant bolls) - 1500 B.C. India origin
  - Flax/Linen (from flax stems)
  - Jute (from jute stems)
  - Hemp, Ramie, Sisal, Kapok, Coir
- **Animal (Protein)**:
  - Wool (from sheep/lambs fleece)
  - Silk (from silkworm cocoons)
  - Angora, Mohair, Camel hair

**Manufactured Fibers:**
- **Natural Polymer**: Viscose, Modal, Diacetate, Triacetate, Lyocell
- **Synthetic Polymer**: 
  - Polyesters
  - Nylon
  - Acrylic
  - Spandex
- **Advanced**: Carbon, Kevlar, Aramid, Glass, Microfibers

### 6.3 Cotton - Properties & Characteristics

**Advantages:**
- Comfortable
- Absorbent
- Good color retention
- Dyes & prints well
- Washable
- Strong
- Drapes well
- Easy to handle and sew
- Inexpensive

**Disadvantages:**
- Shrinks in hot water
- Wrinkles easily
- Weakened by perspiration and sun
- Burns easily
- Affected by mildew

### 6.4 Spinning Systems Evolution

**Manual Spinning (until 1795):**
- Hand-operated process
- Continuous home production

**Machine Spinning:**
- **1795-1838**: Hand-operated machines
- **1838**: Mechanically operated machines
- **Mid-19th century**: Ring spinning machine invented
  - "Ring" = ring part forming yarn

**Modern Spinning Systems (Last 4 decades):**

| System | Year | Yarn Count | Fiber Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Open-End (Rotor) | 1950s | 6-30 | Fine, strong | Different yarn structure |
| Friction | 1960s | Variable | Variable | Dr. Ernest Fehrer |
| Air-Jet | 1970s | 20-60 | Long, strong | Different properties |
| Ring | Traditional | 6-250 | Long, fine, strong | Most common |
| Compact | Modern | 6-250+ | Long, fine, strong | Quality improved |

**Common Types for Apparel:**
- Ring Spinning
- Open End
- Compact
- Air jet

### 6.5 Fabric Types

**Woven Fabrics:**
- Warp threads (longitudinal)
- Weft threads (horizontal)
- Interlaced at right angles

**Weaving History:**
- **12th century**: Wooden hand looms in England
- **1733**: Fly shuttle invented (John Kay) - hand operated
- **1805**: Jacquard system (Josph Jacquard) - individual warp yarn control
- **1835**: Shuttle changing (Reid & Johnson) - multiple colored wefts

**Modern Looms:**
- **Shuttle Looms** (traditional)
  - Problems: Heavy shuttle, low speed, noisy, requires new pirns
  
- **Shuttleless Looms**:
  1. **Projectile Loom (1953)**: Bullet-like gripper carries yarn
     - Advantages: High speed, light gripper forces
     - Disadvantages: Not suited for weak/fine yarns
  
  2. **Rapier Loom (1972)**: Rapier pulls yarn across
     - Single or double rapier
     - Double rapier: 50% of rapier movement utilized
     - Flexible or rigid options
  
  3. **Air-Jet Loom (1982)**: Air carries yarn
     - Faster than projectile/rapier
     - Less yarn-carrying power
     - No abrasion to warp
     - Narrower fabric width
     - Auxiliary nozzles help yarn transport
  
  4. **Water-Jet Loom**: Water propulsion
     - Very efficient
     - Fastest shuttleless loom
     - Requires drying units
     - Best for filament yarns (hydrophobic fibers)

### 6.6 Knitting Technology

**Types of Knitting:**

1. **Weft Knitting** (flat and circular)
   - Yarn feeds horizontally
   - Most common in apparel
   - Modern: Flat knitting machines (CMS 822, SHIMA SEIKI)

2. **Warp Knitting**
   - Yarn sheet linked in longitudinal direction
   - More stable than circular/flat
   - Allows big and small holes
   - Applications: Nets, curtains, technical fabrics, filters
   - Machines: Tricot, Raschel, SANTONI

**Latest Technology:**
- **Full Garment (Whole Garment) Machines**
  - Make complete garments
  - Expensive
  - Applications: Sports wear, automotive, home furnishings, healthcare

### 6.7 Non-Woven Fabrics

**Definition:** Fibers pressed, bonded, or interlocked together using mechanical action, chemicals, and/or heat (no yarn required)

**Production Methods:**

1. **Needle Punching:**
   - Barbed needles mechanically interlock fibers
   - Applications: Blankets, shoe linings, heat/sound insulation, filters, geotextiles

2. **Stitch Bonding:**
   - Stitches bond fiber layers
   - Applications: Geotextiles (roads, aircraft lanes, railways)

3. **Wet-Laid:**
   - Fiber and water slurry on modified paper machine
   - 15% of web production
   - Advantages: Low cost, high production rates
   - Limitations: Short fiber length only
   - Uses: Napkins, aprons, gloves, tea bags, surgical gauzes

**General Applications:**

| Category | Uses |
|---|---|
| Hospital | Surgical gowns, drapes, bed sheets, towels |
| Medical | Wound dressings, gauze, pads, wipes |
| Sanitary | Baby wipes, facial masks, disposable pants |
| Household | Cleaning wipes, table cloths, napkins, curtains |
| Industrial | Filtration, roofing, water insulation, wipes |
| Automotive | Headliners, seat covers, cleaning wipes |
| Interlinings | Fabric stiffening in garments |
| Coating Substrates | Synthetic leather backing |
